After reading 10 Intriguing Photographs to Teach Close Reading and Visual Thinking Skills

It does not sound natural when you say that we read images. However, this article made me realize that when we view any type of photos we are always reading what is happening in the photo and analyzing with our own experiences. Although photo is a type of media that is static and biased, I believe that it provokes viewers' imagination and allows for them to understand different point of views from discussions.

I think that photos are biased in some way because it only shows a small portion of the whole environment and it is from the photographer's perspective. However, this is what makes it great for practicing thinking in others' perspective. It allows for people to think in certain way about the photo and when they discuss it with a group, they could learn something new.

As designers, it is crucial to take different perspectives and empathize with the audiences to fullfill their needs. Not only that, I believe that perspective taking is also crucial for navigating this world since most of media we intake are somewhat biased. So, it is important for us to keep practicing visual literacy skills and protect ourselves from false and exaggerated information.

After reading Best Practices for Modals / Overlays / Dialog Windows

The article goes in detail about what kind of modal design is effective and what is not. Few of the things that I remember is that there needs to be a descriptive title inside the modal, and the buttons need to be carefully worded. Unlike other UI designs for other parts of the website, I realized how wording greatly impacts the quality of user experience when interacting with the modals; users would heavily rely on words to navigate themselves within the modal.

When I have interacted with websites, the modals often displayed subsriptions sign-ups, ongoing sale information, cupon retrieval, and more. The first impression of these modals are similar, but slightly different. Modals like cupons and sales immediately grab my attention and leads me to the specific page that the modal promotes. However, some modals that ask for subscription or sign ups usually negatively impacts my experience with the website, especially when I am in the middle of reading an article.

After reading the article, I realized how small overlay on the screen could greatly determine the first impression of the website. However, now I have a question about timing of modal's appearance. Since surprising users with modals is not a good design, when should the user be introduced to the modals?

After reading Best practices for form design

Reading the article made me consider designing from the users' perspective once again. It is very interesting to think about how small features like multiple columns exhausts users when they fill out forms, and how the location of placeholder text could change users' experience with forms.

Another thing I found intriguing from the article was auto focusing the first input field. I vaguely remember seeing these highlights in my personal interactions with forms, and it is surprising how these small visual cues just pass right through my thoughts. I think it is incredible that it is possible to design something that is so minimal, yet so impactful. After reading about this section, I once again thought that visual cues are much stronger than verbal cues and that it is the designers' job to utilize visiual cues to navigate users.

When I first learned about creating forms, I was only nervous about creating something that I have never thought of creating. However, reading this article made me more enthusiastic about creating a form. I hope that I would be able to utilize information that I learned from this article into my future projects.
